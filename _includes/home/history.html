Gas lights

One of the really unusual features at the Picture House is our gas lighting. These are not the original fittings but they’re still pretty stunning. Very few buildings in the UK now have gas lighting. The only other example we’ve come across is a pub in Beverley near Hull!

The main auditorium

The cinema auditorium is not exactly as it was in 1914 but we are fortunate that no extreme alterations have been made. Many single screen cinemas like the Hyde Park have been converted to bingo halls or carpet warehouses.

Close

The history of the foyer

To imagine the Picture House foyer as it would have been originally, take a good long look at the kiosk area then imagine a fireplace standing in its place! The original floor in the foyer would also have been some form of tiling, but this has long since been irreparably damaged.

The clock

The clock you see to the right of the screen has sat proudly in our auditorium since about the 1950/1960’s. Prior to that it lived in the Gaumont cinema. Len Thompson saved the clock, some seats, and the toilet signs when the Gaumont closed. It hasn’t been knocked down though, oh no. You’ll now know the Gaumont as the O2 Academy.

Decorative glass

The window on the stairs is home to one of our original decorative glass panels. Close inspection reveals it is part stained glass and part hand painted.

Close

Original fire hydrant

While today we’re fully fitted with contemporary fire-fighting equipment, none of that makes quite the same statement as this beauty. Despite its age, this hose is still functional; though since the water comes up from the mains the force is quite impressive – enough to throw the user to the ground.

Our beginnings

After plans for a hotel met local opposition, and the brief residence of a social club, plans for a purpose built cinema on the plot of land at 73 Brudenell Road plans were submitted to Leeds City Council on 6th May 1913, and the Hyde Park Picture House opened for business on 2nd November 1914.

Close

Lamp post

Like the main building the lamppost is Grade II listed. It was created by W. MacFarlane & Co, also known as the Saracen Foundry, renowned at the time as the most important manufacturer of ornamental ironwork in Scotland.

Close

Box office

As you approach our main doors you can’t miss the original box office which is still in use to this day.

Close

Tiles

As you stand, imagining that you’re about to buy some tickets for the latest flick, be sure to look under your feet at the beautiful marble and granite mosaic. Considering the many feet it’s seen over the years it’s in great shape, though you can see that time has been kinder to some sections than others. You can see the green sections of the design are almost worn away!

Close

The first film

The first film to be shown at the cinema was the patriotic drama Their Only Son, followed the next week by the famous invasion drama An Englishman's House. Although few new cinemas would be built during the war-years audiences continued to grow.
